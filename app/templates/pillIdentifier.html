FAQs About Pill Identifier

How do I use the Rx Zee Pill Identifier?

Found a pill that got lost in your medicine cabinet? Do you know someone who mixed up some of their medicines by accident? You might ask, "What pill is this?" for many reasons. The Pill Identifier on Rx Zee can help you name a drug you don't know what it is. It lists prescription and over-the-counter (OTC) drugs that you take by mouth in a solid form, like pills and tablets. The Identifier will tell you what kind of pill it might be after you give some basic information about it. It either shows you a list of matches that are close or picks out one exact match. There is a picture of the pill, its brand and generic names, its strength (dose), and other information in each result. Only FDA-approved medications can be found using the Pill Identifier. It does not identify illegal drugs, food supplements (such as the majority of vitamins and herbals), or drugs from outside the US.

How can I tell if a medicine is over-the-counter (OTC) or prescribed?

You can tell The Pill Identifier any or all of the following:

  • What color is the pill?
  • The way it looks
  • The text on or cut into the pill is called its imprint code.

The most important piece of information is the imprint code, which is often all you need to find a single hit in your search. Since different people may have different ideas about what a pill's color means, you might find it easier to just put the imprint code, shape or no shape of the pill.Your pharmacist can identify a pill using these same information. The pharmacist may also look at the pill's size and cover, as well as for scores, which are line-shaped marks on the pill.

How do the numbers or letters on a pill help me?

A pill's "imprint code" is the set of numbers, letters, or other writing that you see on it. This helps you identify it. An imprint is required by the FDA on most pills and capsules of over-the-counter (OTC) and prescription drugs. This code, along with the size, color, and form of the pill, helps you distinguish it from other medications.

The imprint code on a pill can be any single letter or number, or it can be a mix of letters, numbers, marks, or symbols. It could have words, the name of the company, or other information. The FDA saves the imprint code of a pill in a database that doctors can view after the drug company sends it to the agency.

They say that health care workers can more easily identify a pill if it has a letter or number in its imprint code, so the FDA pushes drugmakers to use those characters. Being able to quickly tell what kind of pill someone is taking could save their life in a medical situation, like an overdose or drug poisoning, where every second counts.

Still, the FDA does not require an imprint on some drugs that are already approved. It thinks about making exceptions for things like

  • Because of how the pill is made, it is impossible to leave an imprint.
  • In a controlled health care setting, such as a doctor's office, health workers administer the pill.
  • People who are sick shouldn't take the medicine by themselves.
How do I get a Pill Imprint Code?

The code is right there on the pill. Some drugs have an imprint code on both the front and back, so look at both. When you use the Pill Identifier to find a pill that has a code on both the front and back, you can enter the code from either side.

Why do different pills with the same drug have different marks on them?

You can tell the difference between the drugs made by different companies by their imprint codes. One type of medicine is sometimes made by more than one company. They might use different inactive ingredients or make it in different amounts. You can also tell the difference between higher and smaller amounts of the same drug made by the same company by their different imprint codes. Also, sometimes more than one generic drug company buys the same drug from the same maker and packages it in their own way. The main part of the pill and its strength will stay the same, though.

Can more than one drug leave the same imprint?

Even if two pills have the same imprint, the general look of each drug must be different from all the others. This includes the size, shape, color, and imprint of each pill. In that way, each one can be correctly named. For instance, the imprint code "N" is on a few different pills, but you can tell them apart by the way their physical features work together. If the Pill Identifier doesn't find a match, check that you're reading the imprint properly. In this case, if you read a "Z" from the wrong angle, it might look like a "N." The same goes for "9" and "6." There are also different ways to use characters that look alike, like "1," "I" (uppercase "i"), and "l" (lowercase "L"). Compare the real pill to the picture in the Pill Identifier results at all times to make sure they are the same, even if the Pill Identifier only gives you one result.

What if a Pill Has No Imprint?

A pill might not have a mark for a number of reasons.

For one thing, the code on the back of a pill might fade over time.

If that didn't happen, the pill might not be a drug that the FDA has cleared. A pill that doesn't have a stamp code could be:

  • Vitamin B
  • Adding on
  • Not allowed drug
  • Medicine from another country

Still, the FDA's stamp code rule doesn't apply to all drugs that are cleared by the agency.

Why is there a line in the middle of some tablets?

On the top of some pills, there is a small hole cut into it. It's known as a "score." It tells you where to cut or split a tablet. There is only one score mark on some screens. Some people have more than one. Since some tablets don't have score lines, cutting them might not be safe. You should only break a tablet if your shopkeeper or doctor tells you to and shows you how. To change the amount of your medicine, they may ask you to split your pills. They might also tell you to do it to save money, since some double-strength pills cost about the same as lower-dose ones. If your doctor or pharmacist tells you to cut your pills in half, ask them if you should use a "tablet splitter" or "pill cutter." It can help you get the right amount. Even so, a tablet splitter might not be the best tool for the job because different pills are not all the same size or shape. Make sure you talk to your doctor or pharmacist first. Just split the pill you're about to take. But don't cut them all at once. Heat, humidity, and moisture can change the way split pills work when you store them. Talk to your doctor or chemist about whether it's safe to split the new tablet if you switch brands of the same medicine. There's a chance it wasn't made the same way as your old brand, so it might not be safe to split.

Who Do I Call to Find Out What Kind of Pill I Have?

Calling your pharmacist is the best thing to do if you've mixed up your pills and need to get them back in order. The pills should be easy to find because of their color, shape, and ID number. To help, they can also see your drug records. If you believe someone has overdosed or been poisoned by an unknown pill, you should call 911 or the toll-free number for your local poison center at 800-222-1222. As a general rule, you should never take pills that you find lying around or that were not given to you, even if you know what they are. It's impossible to know what else they've been near, if they've been messed with, or if they were kept correctly. If you need to know how to get rid of prescription drugs, read our piece.
