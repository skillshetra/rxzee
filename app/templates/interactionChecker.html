FAQs About Drug Interactions

What is the best website to check drug interactions?

Up to 4 drugs can be checked against each other at once on Rx Zee to see if they might mix with each other. If an interaction is found, the pair of drugs that caused it is listed along with a seriousness level: minor.

What is drug interactions checker?

To find out more, you can also use the Rx Zee Drug Interaction Checker. This tool tells you what the contact is, how it happens, how important it is, and how to deal with it. It will also show you if your chosen drugs, foods, drinks, or medical situation might combine with each other.

What is a drug interaction?

You should look out for three main types of drug interactions:

Interactions between drugs: Some drugs can combine with each other in this way, which happens most of the time. This kind of combination is more likely to happen if you take a lot of drugs. For instance, this side effect can get worse if you take two drugs that make you sleepy at the same time.

Drugs and food and drinks that interact: When drugs are mixed with food or drinks, they can change how they work or make side effects worse. For instance, orange juice and statins can cause muscle pain, and booze and painkillers can make breathing too slowly, which is very dangerous.

Drug reactions with diseases: Your health problems may change how a drug works or cause side effects. Say you already have high blood pressure, taking a nasal suppressant like pseudoephedrine might make it worse.

What are common signs and symptoms of a drug interaction?

There are a lot of different ways that a drug combination can show up. Some signs might not be very bad, but others could be very bad or even deadly.

Some common signs or symptoms of a drug combination are:

  • Being sleepy
  • Feeling sick
  • Feeling sick or vomiting
  • Having stress or being antsy
  • You might not be getting as much from your medicine.
You should talk to your doctor right away if you think a symptom or side effect you're having might be caused by a drug combination.
How can I avoid drug interactions?

It's important to check for drug combinations before you start treatment because you might not know if your medicines mix with each other.

Here are some ways to keep drugs from interacting with each other:

  1. Not only should you tell your doctor about the prescription drugs you take, but also about any over-the-counter (OTC) drugs you take, like vitamins, plant supplements, or food supplements.
  2. You might find it useful to bring a list of your medicines with you to the store and doctor's visits.
  3. Talk to your doctor and chemist about the drugs you take. Find out why you are taking it, how often, and if you should not take it with other drugs, booze, food, or drinks.
  4. Ask your doctor or nurse to look over your medications to see if they combine with any other drugs in a way that is important. Figure out how to spot the effects of any drug problems.
  5. Find out when you should call your doctor or 911 for help in case of a drug problem.

There are written directions that come with all meds. Carefully follow those steps. There is also a sticker on over-the-counter drugs called "Drug Facts" that helps you understand the medicine. Take your time reading this. If you don't understand what to do, ask a health care worker for help.

There are two kinds of responses to medicines: The most common response to drugs is an adverse (non-allergic) reaction. You can have a real reaction to drugs, which means your immune system is involved.

What Drugs Can You Not Take Together?

There are a lot of different kinds of drugs that you shouldn't take together, but here are some examples:

More than one drug that has the same active ingredient. You might have side affects or take too much. The chemicals in medicines that help your disease or symptoms are called active ingredients. Always look for them on the box of the medicine.
Drugs that thin the blood with NSAIDs. There is a chance that you will bleed in a dangerous way. Nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs, or NSAIDs, are pain killers like naproxen or ibuprofen. Tell your doctor about any over-the-counter painkillers you're taking that might not be safe for you if you are on a blood thinner.
Pills that contain antihistamines. Taking these together might make you move more slowly, which would be unsafe if you drive or work with big machinery.

Another group of drugs that can be dangerous together is:

  • A pain killer with an NSAID and a blood pressure medicine
  • A drug for the thyroid called levothyroxine and a drug for stomach acid called omeprazole
  • Taking an SSRI antidepressant along with a serotonin-affecting drug (like dextromethorphan, linezolid, tramadol, or trazodone)
  • An antidiabetic and fibrate drug that lowers cholesterol. These drugs are called "azole" drugs, but their scientific names are "imidazole and triazole derivatives." They lower lipids, which are fats in the blood.
  • Taking clarithromycin with a calcium channel blocker, which is a type of blood pressure medicine

How to Treat Drug Allergies at Home
You might be able to get over-the-counter medicines that can help with mild drug allergy signs like hives or itchiness at home. When it comes to skin responses, cool cloths can ease the pain and calamine cream can help with itchiness and rashes.

What Are Common Drug Interactions?
Some medicines don't work well with certain drinks and foods. Here are some of them:
Liquor. Getting drunk can make many medicines less safe, including some used to:

  • Allergies
  • The flu and cold
  • High blood sugar
  • Having high blood pressure
  • ADHD, or attention deficit hyperactivity disorder
  • Feeling down
  • Diabetes
  • Getting sick
  • Pain
  • Not getting enough sleep


Ask your doctor or pharmacist if a new drug might combine with booze before you start taking it. Also, read the package to see if there are any drinking alerts.

Grapefruit or drink made from grapefruit. These drugs can be harmed by too much of either:
  • Statins
  • Pain killers
  • Drugs for high blood pressure
  • Drugs that stop transplant rejection
  • Anxiety meds


There are some types of drugs in these groups that don't react badly with grapefruit juice. Look at the sticker or information leaflet on your medicine to see if there are any warnings. Also, check with your doctor or pharmacist to see if it's okay to have any amount of grapefruit or grapefruit juice with your medicine. If they tell you not to eat or drink it, ask if any other fruits or sauces might affect your medicine in the same way.

Things that have vitamin K in them, like leafy greens. These things can affect how the blood thinner warfarin works. You don't have to stop eating them, but you should be careful not to eat too many. Talk to your doctor about how much vitamin K-rich food you can eat. Then, every week, eat the same amount around the same times.

Some foods and drinks that are high in potassium are orange juice, bananas, and salt alternatives. These can change the way blood pressure medicines called ACE inhibitors work. A lot of foods have potassium, so your doctor may tell you to eat less of those foods.

St. John's Wort. Many medicines for heart disease, HIV, sadness, and other diseases can be affected by this plant product. Drugs used to treat cancer, such as irinotecan and imatinib, can also be affected.

Before you try a new product, you should always check with your doctor or pharmacist first. Also, a drug you're taking might not work well with a health problem you already have. Some common conditions that combine with drugs are:
  • Antacids and kidney disease
  • Antihistamines and some lung issues, cataracts, and a bigger prostate
  • Inhalers that help people with asthma and heart disease, high blood pressure, kidney disease, and diabetes
  • Cold medicines and health problems like diabetes, heart disease, high blood pressure, and thyroid problems
  • Sleep aids and some lung issues, cataracts, and a bigger prostate

How Do Drug Interactions Happen?

Taking two medicines at different times of the day might not stop them from mixing, and it could cause new or worse issues. Your doctor may need to change your amount or give you a different drug to avoid a reaction.Several things can happen when drugs are mixed together. This list shows some of the most popular ways.

If you take two drugs that can cause the same side effect at the same time, it may get worse. Say two drugs can each make you sleepy. Taking them together can make you even sleepier, which can be risky.

It's possible to get less of the effects you want when the bad effects of one drug are the same as the good effects of another. For instance, taking one drug that makes your blood pressure go up may make the effects of another drug that lowers your blood pressure less effective.

The bowels are where most drugs that you take get into your blood. Another drug may not be able to be taken if it is blocked or stuck in the intestines by another drug. For instance, vitamins like iron and calcium can stop thyroid medicines from working.

The cytochrome p450 (CYP) and other enzymes in your body break down many types of medicines. The name for this is metabolism. Also, it can get rid of drugs, most of the time through pee. These processes may be sped up, slowed down, or even stopped by other drugs. The amount of drug in your body may go up (like taking too much) or down (like taking too little) when this happens. This could lead to big trouble either way.
